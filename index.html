Coral bleaching

When corals are stressed by changes in conditions such as temperature,
light, or nutrients, they expel the symbiotic algae living in their tissues, causing them to turn completely white.

What Is It?

What Is It?

The skeletal structure of hard corals are normally white, but they have color due to zooxanthellae algae. These tiny plants, which are a type of dinoflagellates (single-celled microscopic organisms that belong to the Protista kingdom), reside within the soft tissues of corals. These microalgae are photosynthetic, and they have an endosymbiotic relationship with some corals, as well as other marine life such as Tridacnid clams, nudibranchs, some sponges, and even jellyfish. Symbiosis refers to two dissimilar organisms living together in a mutually beneficial relationship.

What Causes It?

What Causes It?

If the rate of photosynthetic production is too high, corals have the ability to control the number of zooxanthellae in their tissues by expelling it. This is known as coral bleaching, which is normal. However, when corals are immoderately stressed, this causes them to expel more zooxanthellae than necessary, and therefore loss of color results from the expulsion of too many zooxanthellae, and/or the concentration of photosynthetic pigments in these organisms are diminished. Although long-term bleaching can cause the partial or total death of coral colonies, if the situation is not too severe and stressful conditions are changed, it is possible for affected colonies to recover their symbiotic microalgae and start growing again. .

How Does It Impact Marine Ecosystems?

Coral bleaching has a wide variety of impacts, including:

๐ Increase/decrease in water temperatures.
๐ Increase/decrease in water salinity.
๐ Increase/decrease in air temperatures.
๐ A build-up of carbon dioxide and methane gasses.
๐ Exposure to increased ultraviolet radiation.
๐ Exposure to high light levels.
๐ Increased or high water turbulence.
๐ Decreased levels of light.
